Topic & Phenomenon
What real-world thing do you want to understand?
▼
—
Self-check — mark what's honestly true right now
What this stage produces
  • A plain-language, specific description of the phenomenon
  • A clear sense of why it matters — your motivation for the project
  • Enough clarity to start productive literature searching
Questions to push yourself
  • Is this a phenomenon or just a topic? (See pitfall below.)
  • Can you describe it to a non-expert in two sentences?
  • Have you observed this in the real world, or only read about it?
  • What about it genuinely puzzles or bothers you?
  • Do you have a sense of the context you want to study this in — a specific industry, platform, brand, or population? It doesn't need to be locked in yet, but having a context in mind will focus your literature search.
!
Common pitfall — confusing a topic with a phenomenon
"Consumer behavior" is a field. "Sustainable consumption" is a topic. "Why consumers who hold strong environmental values still choose fast fashion when shopping under time pressure" is a phenomenon — specific, observable, and puzzling. Push until you have something at that level of specificity. If you can't describe it in two sentences without using words like "the relationship between" or "the impact of," keep sharpening.
You're ready to move to Stage 2 when...
  • You can describe your phenomenon in two specific, jargon-free sentences
  • You can name 2–3 real-world examples of it
  • You can explain why it matters without saying "I find it interesting"
In your own words: what is the phenomenon you want to study?
Aim for two sentences. Avoid jargon. Bring this to your advising meeting.

Literature Review
What do we already know? What gaps remain?
▼
—
Self-check — mark what's honestly true right now
Where and how to search
Google Scholar UA Library (full text) NotebookLM Zotero
  • Start broad, then narrow. Search your phenomenon + the discipline (e.g., "linguistic relativity consumer behavior," "language perception marketing")
  • Follow citations. The reference lists of good papers are your best next leads
  • You may need to bridge two literatures. Some phenomena live at the intersection of fields — search both
  • Use NotebookLM to synthesize themes across PDFs once you have 6+ papers
  • Rule of thumb: Read intros and conclusions of everything you cite; read the middle of the papers that matter most
What you're looking for
  • What do scholars broadly agree on?
  • Where do findings conflict or remain inconclusive?
  • What contexts, populations, or angles are understudied?
  • What do conclusions say "future research should explore"?
  • What theoretical frameworks keep appearing?
  • Are there adjacent fields that study this differently?
  • Is the literature specific to your context (a particular industry, platform, or population) or does it study the phenomenon more generally? Both matter — but you'll eventually need to understand both the general phenomenon and how it plays out in your specific context.
!
Common pitfall — summarizing instead of synthesizing
A lit review is not a list of what each paper found. It's an argument about what the field collectively knows, where it disagrees, and what remains unanswered. Organize your thinking by theme or tension, not by paper. Ask: if you removed all the author names and dates, would your summary still make a coherent argument? It should.
You're ready to move to Stage 3 when...
  • You can identify 2–3 themes or debates in the literature without looking at your notes
  • You can name at least one specific, honest gap — not just "more research is needed"
  • A research question is beginning to suggest itself from the gap you've found
↺

Even a rough intuition counts. What keeps coming up as unresolved, understudied, or contested?

Research Question
What specific gap is your study addressing?
▼
—
Self-check — mark what's honestly true right now
What makes a strong research question
  • Starts with "how," "why," or "what" — not yes/no
  • Specific enough to answer in a single study
  • Clearly rooted in a gap you found in the literature
  • Answerable with realistic access and methods
  • Connects back to your original phenomenon
  • Names a specific context — the field, industry, platform, brand, or population you're studying. Context is what makes a question answerable rather than endless.
Types of research questions
  • Descriptive: What does X look like? How common is Y?
  • Exploratory: Why does X happen? What shapes Y?
  • Explanatory: How does X lead to Y? What is the mechanism?
  • Comparative: How does X differ across contexts or groups?
!
Common pitfall — forcing a question before the lit review is ready
A research question should feel like it falls out of a thorough literature review — not like something you invented before you read enough. If your question doesn't connect directly to a gap you can name from specific papers, you likely need more reading first. Premature questions tend to be either too broad to answer or disconnected from what's actually missing in the field.
You're ready to move to Stage 4 when...
  • Your question is one sentence, open-ended, and you can write it from memory
  • Your question names a specific context — not just a phenomenon in the abstract
  • You can name the specific papers or debates that revealed the gap your question addresses
  • Your advisor has seen and responded to at least a draft of your question
Draft your research question here — even a rough version:
Start with "How does..." or "Why do..." or "What is the relationship between..." — then tighten from there.

Theory
What lens helps explain your phenomenon?
▼
—
Self-check — mark what's honestly true right now
What theory does for your thesis
  • Provides principled explanation, not just description
  • Anchors your findings in broader scholarly conversations
  • Shapes how you interpret your data — actively, not decoratively
  • Makes your contribution legible to other researchers
  • Bridges your specific findings back to general knowledge
How to find your theory
  • Look at what frameworks appear repeatedly in your most useful papers
  • Ask your advisor what theories are prominent in this space
  • Check if theories from adjacent fields apply — cross-disciplinary lenses can be powerful
  • Note: some theories are controversial or have limited empirical support — know your theory's standing in the literature
!
Common pitfall — treating theory as decoration
Naming a theory in your intro and never returning to it is not theoretical grounding. Your theory should be woven through your entire thesis: it shapes what you look for in data, how you interpret what you find, and how you situate your contribution in the conclusion. If you can delete all mentions of the theory without changing your argument, it isn't doing any work.
You're ready to move to Stage 5 when...
  • You can explain your theory in plain language without looking it up
  • You can articulate how the theory connects directly to your research question
  • You know the theory's limitations and can acknowledge them honestly
What theoretical lens are you considering — and why does it fit?
Name the theory, explain what it says, and explain why it helps make sense of your specific phenomenon.

Method
How will you collect and analyze data?
▼
—
Self-check — mark what's honestly true right now
Common approaches
  • Interviews / focus groups — rich qualitative depth
  • Surveys / experiments — patterns across larger samples
  • Content / discourse analysis — texts, ads, documents, media
  • Case studies — deep contextual understanding
  • Secondary data analysis — existing datasets or archives
Feasibility questions to answer
  • Who or what is your data source — and can you realistically access them?
  • Does this method require IRB approval? (Talk to your advisor early.)
  • What unique access, language skills, or experience do you bring?
  • What does a realistic timeline look like for data collection + analysis?
!
Common pitfall — choosing method before question
"I want to do interviews" is a format preference, not a method decision. Your research question should drive the method — not the other way around. Ask yourself: what is the most honest way to find out what my question is asking? If your question asks why something happens, you probably need a method that captures reasoning and context. If it asks how often or to what extent, you likely need a method that reaches more people.
You're ready to move to Stage 6 when...
  • Method is decided and you can explain why it fits your research question
  • You have a realistic data access plan — not just a theoretical one
  • IRB status is confirmed with your advisor
How do you think you'd actually study this — and what's your biggest constraint?
Be honest about what's difficult. Access, time, expertise — naming the constraint is the first step to solving it.
6
Findings & Conclusion
What will you have learned — and why will it matter?
▼
—
Self-check — mark what's honestly true right now
What strong findings look like
  • Directly answer the research question
  • Organized by themes or patterns — not by data source or timeline
  • Traceable back to evidence — the reader can see how you got there
  • Neither overstated nor undersold
What strong conclusions do
  • Interpret — say what the findings mean, not just what they are
  • Situate findings in the literature you reviewed
  • Name limitations honestly — this is integrity, not weakness
  • Articulate a clear, modest contribution: what do we now know?
  • Suggest where the research conversation goes next
!
Common pitfall — restating findings as the conclusion
A conclusion that only summarizes what you found isn't a conclusion — it's a recap. The conclusion zooms back out: it situates your findings in the broader literature, it says what we now know that we didn't before, and it opens the door to what comes next. The funnel narrows — and then the conclusion cracks it open again.
If this thesis goes well, what will you have contributed?
Try finishing: "After this study, we will know ___ that we didn't know before..." Even a rough answer here clarifies your purpose.
